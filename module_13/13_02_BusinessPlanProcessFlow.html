Business Plan Process Flow

Notice the location and direction of the arrows between the differing planning and capacity processes.

These processes all work together in a system of checks and balances. At each planning level, the capacity decision(s) (sideways arrows to decisions) must be verified before moving down to the next planning level. Wherever an issue occurs, it may affect the adjacent upper and lower planning levels.

For example, if there is a material shortage at the MRP stage. This capacity issue affects both the planning of the finished goods (MPS) and the manufacture (PAC). The resolution of the MRP issue will likely result in changes in one or both of the MPS and PAC stages. These must again be verified via their associated capacity decisions or processes before proceeding.

Strategic Business Planning

This strategic part of the plan is created by the highest level of senior management. They are concerned with how new product lines and/or markets deliver cash flow and increase the bottom line of the company. In this 2-10 year plan, building size, what and how products or services will be produced, and location(s) are the important capacity concerns for most firms in either service or manufacturing sectors.

Sales and Operations Planning (S & OP)

This is now a Tactical plan which is managed in monthly time buckets 1 to 2 years out in the planning horizon. Here we deal with product families, which can be aggregated (put together) from the end items in the Master Production Schedule.

Example: 1 % milk is the product family, and it is available in 250 ml, 750 ml, 1 litre and 2 litre cartons as well as 4 litre bags. Here is where we determine the overall aggregate strategy. Will we use level, chase or mixed (details later) production strategies? What will be the overall inventory levels, employment levels and will it be warehoused, shipped from and by whom (logistics)? All supply chain activities and functional roles in the company must be co-ordinated in the S & OP process and support the Business Plan above.

Master Production Scheduling (MPS)

In this tactical level, we can finally plan to build individual finished goods in weekly time buckets for which the customer can place an order. These independent demand items are forecast and represent what and when the company plans to produce in specific configurations, quantities and dates. The MPS is the interface for sales forecasts, actual customer orders and scheduled production. These are intermediate range plans going out into the future a minimum of time periods (weeks) to cover the longest cumulative planning lead time plus one time bucket. The product family, 1 % milk, in the S & OP can be dis-aggregated into the end items in this MPS. Refer to the burger family in your Custom Chapter for another example using numbers. The critical resource capacity decisions may include changes to one or more of workforce, shifts, overtime, inventory levels, material supply, logistics decisions – whatever is crucial to the process.

Material Requirements Planning (MRP)

What have we not done yet? It is time to order the components. This final step in the planning phase is short range (daily) and the most detailed. These dependent demand items are planned usually with the aid of computerized planning systems. These systems use bill of material (BOM) data, inventory data and the MPS to calculate the requirements for the materials. It is critical for this data and information to be accurate for the system output of a planned order to be useful throughout the supply chain. The time frame varies considerably depending on the industry. The flow chart indicates a time frame of up to 2 months but this is much longer in aerospace and power generation for example. Capacity planning is very detailed at this point, involving the calculation of system requirements for each activity in the manufacturing process.

Purchasing & Production Activity Control (PAC)

We are now in the Execution and Control phase. Purchasing and Production Activity Control (PAC) are working to meet the customer requirements.

Purchasing sends any required planned orders from MRP to the suppliers. Follow-up and possible expediting (speeding up orders) will take place as required.

PAC uses any in-house planned orders (already available) and ensures shop floor manufacturing takes place as planned, identify any problems, and take immediate corrective action. Continuous improvement is especially critical in PAC to drive out process variation and improve overall supply chain performance.

A closed loop feedback system on the shop floor (PAC) and vendor performance (Purchasing) are keys for this stage to be effective. This feedback must be communicated to the other business process levels via the MRP.

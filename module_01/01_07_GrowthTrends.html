Growth in services and current trends in Operations Management

In the early 1900s, about two-thirds of the working population were employed directly in the goods-producing section… mainly agriculture.

Now, with the increasing efficiency and productivity in the manufacturing and agricultural sectors Statistics Canada (Statistics Canada, 2006) says three out of every four working Canadians—12 million people—work in the services sector. It noted that there has been sharp growth in technology-intensive services such as logistics and information and communications technology…all important in the operations fields.

I want you to think about the trends in Operations Management. In particular, consider the role of information and technology in helping these changes become reality today. Expand the panels below to learn more.

Local or National Focus vs. Global Focus
Communication and transportation cost drops have made markets global. Think about how much more you travel today vs. the itinerary for your parents or grandparents. Think about the exchange of ideas, designs, information and how it is so easy and inexpensive to now communicate globally.
Batch Shipments vs. Just-in-Time
Earlier a truck was shipped when full. Now, consolidation and electronic updates on shipments means inventories are kept low and stock arrives when needed.
Lengthy Product Development vs. Rapid Product Development
New products use to have two or three year development times. Now, improved technology and demanding customers means supply chain partners working together to cut out the unnecessary steps can have months vs. years between new product announcements.
Job specialization vs. Empowered employees and self-directed teams
Organization charts use to be multi-layered but more and more we read of companies today where companies are managed by self-directed teams. Check out this recent story by the BBC News about a Swedish company where nobody is in charge. (BBC, 2017)
Standard Products vs. Mass customization
Gone are the days when Ford (1909) announced that he was going to build only one model and “any customer can have a car painted any colour that he wants so long as it is black.” (Hart, 2015) Today, companies can change production and volume levels and instead make custom-made products with the low unit costs associated in the past with mass production (Investopedia, n.d.).
Low cost production with little concern for the environment vs. environmentally sensitive production
The public is now much more sensitive to the environment. Standards are now in place for ISO14000 and increasingly companies focus on better disposal, energy needs and products used in production.
Ethics and regulations not at the forefront vs. Ethics, CSR, and Triple Bottom Line
These are now part of any business decision. Gone are the days when cheapest is best. Now companies worry about the customers’ views on the choices made by the company in its supply chain so companies work towards achieving a good Triple Bottom Line (People, Planet, Profits).

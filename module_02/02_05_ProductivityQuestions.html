A team of 3 clerks stocked 24 shelves in an hour. What was their productivity?
= 24/3 = 8 shelves stocked per hour
A second team of 2 stocked 40 shelves in two hours. What was their productivity?
= 40 / (2 x 2) = 40/4 = 10 shelves stocked/hour
Who was more productive? What was the percentage difference in their productivity per hour?
= new-old/old = 10 – 8/8 = 2/8 or the second team is 25% more productive.

Points to Consider

Does this mean that Team 2 was more efficient?

No. Efficiency would be measured Actual Productivity / Standard Productivity.

Should you give Team 2 a bonus for being more productive?

Not necessarily… there might be other reasons they were more productive.

Think of three reasons Team 2 may have been more productive

Qualitative Considerations

Always think about qualitative considerations when examining productivity calculations. You need to measure “apples to apples”.

1

Quality of the productivity may change

Sloppy work… maybe the faster team was not careful to face all boxes and cans to the front and would later require rework.

2

Various External Elements may cause an increase or decrease in productivity

Maybe one of the restockers was tall and could reach top shelves without going up and down on stools.

There was less interference by customers--maybe the faster team was working overnight when there are fewer customers crowding the aisles and asking questions.

Experience – one team could be more experienced with the task and the layout of the store.

3

Precise units of measure may be lacking

The size of items may vary (boxes of cereal vs. jars of spices).

Area being stocked is closer to the storeroom where they picked up stock.

With a capital investment, one team had a hand-cart and brought out a pallet of stock at a time.

…and so on.

Multifactor productivity

Multifactor productivity measures the relationship of outputs to all inputs and is usually expressed as units per dollar spent. This is more complicated but it does provide a better representation of the productivity as it includes all the costs connected with the increase in output.

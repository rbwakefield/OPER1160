Business Ethics and Corporate Social Responsibility

The quest for competitive advantage in supply chains can have serious impacts on the way products are made, how waste is disposed of or how workers are treated.

Think about the conflicting priorities of stakeholders such as customers, distributors, suppliers, owners, lenders, and employees. But today, companies are doing a better job of auditing their actions and global impacts. Many, such as Magna International have now placed the needs of society high on the priority list and publicly declared corporate social responsibility is important. (Lopers et al., 2016, p. 21)

In the 1990s, Nike was the poster-child for outsourcing manufacturing operations to a cheaper location that had labour exploitation issues. A few years ago, the Bangladesh tragedy in the garment industry that killed over 1,000 workers opened our eyes. Human rights groups around the world made corporations realize something should be done. We can only hope that tragedies like this were not in vain.

Operations and Productivity

Productivity and Variables
That Affect Productivity Calculations

Productivity is about how we combine resources to produce goods and services.

The more efficiently we do this, the more productive we are and the more value is added to the good or service produced. Only through increases in productivity can the standard of living improve. It is all about creating more from our available resources. And, generally speaking, the higher the productivity of a country, the higher the living standards that it can afford. Those higher living standards mean things like better educational and health systems, better infrastructure and safer communities with higher environmental standards.

This can be accomplished in two ways: reducing inputs while keeping output constant, or increasing output while keeping inputs constant. Productivity variables are things like labour, capital and management.

Refer back to your readings on the OM in Action: Improving productivity at Starbucks.
(Lopers et al., 2016, p. 16)

What were three ways Productivity was improved at Starbucks?

They stopped requiring signatures on credit card purchases under $25…saved 8 seconds per transaction.

They redesigned the size of the ice scoop and saved 14 seconds per iced drink.

They installed new espresso machines and saved 12 seconds per shot.

Productivity Calculations

Let’s look at some single factor productivity calculations – the only ones we’ll do in this course. The measurement of productivity can be simple where we measure the relationship of outputs to the labour-hours put in. Single-factor Productivity = Outputs / Inputs and is expressed as outputs per labour-hour. This single-factor productivity calculation is easy – just remember to capture the total labour hours.

Productivity =

Units Produced

Input Used

For example, if one person completes 6 service calls in 2 hours;

Productivity = # of service calls completed (outputs) / Labour hours used (inputs)

= 6/2 = 3 service calls per hour.

If two people work together for 2 hours to make 16 service calls… that’s a total of 4 labour-hours;

= 16/(2 x 2) = 4 service calls per labour-hour.

What was the change in productivity?

Take the (new productivity – old productivity) / old productivity

= 4 – 3 / 3 = 1/3 or 33% increase in productivity

Hint: the old or original goes on the bottom…think of it as the base.

Consider a paper-route. Forty papers can be delivered in 20 minutes when walking (2 papers delivered per minute) but with a car, the time could be reduced to 5 minutes. (8 papers delivered per minute) but the car cost is far more than the shoe-leather.

Important Note! Productivity is a measure of output effectiveness and NOT a measure of efficiency. Efficiency is the Actual Productivity / Standard Productivity.

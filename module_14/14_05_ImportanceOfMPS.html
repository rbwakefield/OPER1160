Business Planning Process II

Importance of Master Production Scheduling (MPS)

Remember from the previous unit, the MPS is where we create an anticipated build schedule of finished independent demand items to satisfy customer demand.

The MPS is the primary interface and coordination point for forecasted demand, actual customer orders, and any adjustments needed from current information from the production area. Here we can disaggregate (break down) the S & OP Plan that was at the product family grouping into the individual finished goods MPS.

Available to Promise (ATP) is a tool that is used in this stage to know how much product is still available to commit to new customer orders. The ATP is the calculation that tells you how much planned uncommitted inventory will be available in each time period.

At this point, scheduling has been the focus. Now, Rough-Cut Capacity Planning (RCCP) must be done in the MPS stage to verify capacity is available for our critical resources. When verified, then the MPS must be firmed (moving from planned to scheduled) before ordering the components via the MRP.

This stage is where the forecasted plus actual demand and available capacity must be aligned.

In the service industry, you cannot have finished services in inventory but you can have people and equipment available when the customer requires the service. Think about how many grocery store checkouts are open and staffed before statutory holidays. Now it is time to order the components or materials.

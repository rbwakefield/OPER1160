Business Planning Process II

Production Planning Strategies

At the S&OP level, companies will need to decide if they want to carry inventory and produce the same amount each day with little or no overtime OR will they chase the demand and produce what is needed when it is needed.

Let’s look at both of these strategies.

Level Production

Click for a larger version

Figure 1: Level Production Plan. Taken from Lopers, pg. 8 (2009)

In this strategy production is held constant while inventory varies. You want to keep your resource level steady (labour, machines, materials) as you might have highly skilled employees or expensive equipment that should not sit idle. Employees are usually happy if they have about the same amount of work each week and do not have to worry about layoffs. You may also want to protect those employees because of their training. There are significant costs associated with layoffs, hiring and training which you can eliminate with level production. Happy workers result in satisfied customers. The negative side is that you will have varying inventory levels and the associated carrying costs (Unit # 7). Figure 5 in the Custom Chapter (Lopers, T., 2009, p. 9) is an example of a level production planning spreadsheet including cost information.

What are some examples of businesses that use a level strategy?

MTD
lawnmowers and snowblowers produced in offsetting seasons
Bombardier
ski-doos and sea-doos
Toyota
shutting down production lines is expensive – plan set 3 months in advance
Your College
more summer courses and programs are available to even out the year round schedule

Chase Production

Click for a larger version

Figure 2: Chase Production plan. Taken from Lopers, pg. 11 (2009)

This planning strategy chases or matches production levels to the sales forecast. As you build no inventory, you have to produce on demand as in most service industries. We will look at options for service industries in the next section. The Chase strategy works well when you can afford to have idle equipment, your labour is easily trained, and comes from a large labour pool (agency). At least inventory can be set and maintained at a predetermined level. Figure 7 in the Custom Chapter (Lopers, T., 2009, p. 12) is an example of a chase production planning spreadsheet including cost information.

What are some examples of businesses that use a chase strategy?

College bookstore
longer hours and extra staff first week of semester
UPS
hires many thousands of workers for peak seasons
Farmers
planting and harvesting are weather sensitive and cannot wait
Post office
brings in workers for each shift from an agency based on volume to be processed

Mixed Production

Most common in business is a combination of Level and Chase. The problem is that it takes a few tries to come up with an optimum plan. Then, when the inputs change the next month, the team has to start all over again. However, it does usually result in the lowest overall cost.

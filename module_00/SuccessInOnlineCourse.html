How to Succeed in an Online Course

One advantage to an online course is that you can do the learning at whatever time fits your schedule. This course is designed to be asynchronous, which means that participants will come and go throughout the week(s) to do the work, but everyone will arrive to the same point by the end of each unit.

I recommend spreading your time in the course over a few different days in the week since people will participate at different times, especially on a discussion board. Graded discussion boards have a requirement that you respond to at least one post authored by another student. If you are on the course only on Tuesday from 6:30 to 9:30, for example, you would miss out on a potentially rich conversation.

In general, as an online student, you should:

  • Access your course regularly depending upon your course requirements. Read the course Getting Started sections so that you are clear on how often you are expected to access your course. If not specifically stated, ensure you access your course a minimum of once per week from the course commencement date. Any changes to your course will be communicated through the online course only (not by telephone or mail)
  • Do no work too far ahead, as the content in future weeks may change
  • Communicate effectively. Be polite and respectful Participate in class discussions
  • Be self-motivated. Manage time effectively Submit assignments by the deadlines established by your course instructor
  • Have access to the necessary equipment to participate in an online course. Computer, modem and any specialized software ISP (Internet Service Provider)
  • Login to your course by the course start date
  • Use the technology properly. Review available training modules Ask for help when needed
  • Be open-minded to sharing life, work and education experiences as part of the learning process.
  • Be willing and able to commit the time necessary to successfully complete the course. (time varies from course to course)
  • Ask questions if you do not understand.
  • Desk stretches You may find yourself sitting at your computer or work table for long periods of time. This can be very hard on your neck, back, and leg muscles. Be sure to take frequent breaks and stretch your muscles.

Remember: to be a successful on-line learner, you should:

  • Be committed to completing your course of study
  • Have good reading and comprehension skills
  • Be able to overcome distractions
  • Be able to commit time to your studies
  • Have quiet study space available
  • Have confidence in yourself as an independent learner

Group Work

Working in groups can be a rewarding experience. When you share knowledge, resources and cases, you are able to enrich the learning of your peers. Health care teams depend on one another to enhance patient care, so many of the assignments in this program will require group work.

By demonstrating you can effectively work in a group during this course, you will ensure you are prepared to collaborate as a team when out in the community providing care. Be respectful of one another and value the contributions of each member of your group; work as a team.

Enrolling in a Group

Once you enroll in a group, you will get access to the group discussion board and group dropboxes.

1. From the Course Tools menu on the course page, select Groups.

2. Click on Choose Group.

3. To choose a group, click on the button beside one of the group names (1). In order to see who is already enrolled in a group, click on the blue number to the left of the group (under Members) (2).

Click on Select to finish the process. You are now enrolled in a group!

Citations

If you are using an idea (or words or image) you got from another source, you will need to say where you got it from. Each program will have different requirements, but for the Enhanced Personal Support Worker you simply need to provide the name of the author (or association) and a few words from the title to make it clear which source you are using. If it is a multi-page document, including the page number will help your peers find your source easier, for example:

"The RNAO (Establishing Therapeutic Relationships, p. 20) suggested that health care workers should reflect on their personal practice in order to talk to patients better. I do this in my practice by..."

Procure-to-Pay Cycle (P2P)

When agreements have been reached and contracts are signed, then it is time to order the product or service. The five main steps in the P2P cycle are:

Open All / Close All
Step 1: Ordering

A company will usually use a Purchase Order (PO) or Blanket Purchase Order to order. It lays out the details.

A Blanket Purchase Order is an agreement that allows a company to order goods or services at a predetermined price up to a total ceiling amount/quantity in smaller, multiple deliveries over a set period of time (usually a year). There is no need to issue a new, separate PO each time…each order is covered under the Blanket PO.

Step 2: Follow-Up and Expediting

Once ordered, it becomes an Open Order and someone must monitor it to ensure it is fulfilled and delivered. If needed quicker, expediting, or hurrying up the shipment often means paying a premium.

Step 3: Receipt and Inspection

For products, when items are received they likely include a packing slip that shows details of the shipment (excluding the price) like quantity ordered, quantity shipped, any backorders or changes in quantity or item ordered. At the receiving dock it is important to verify the details of the shipment and note any discrepancies on the Packing Slip before sending up to the Accounts Payable.

For services, suppliers will provide a timesheet or a statement of work or scope of work (SOW) will be provided and the receiving company will sign off or note any required modifications before it is sent on to the Accounts Payable.

Step 4: Settlement and Payment

Once the shipment or service details have been verified and signed off as satisfactory, the Accounts Payable department will check the packing slip or SOW against the original Purchase Order. If all is in order they will authorize a payment. Often, electronic funds transfers (EFTs) are arranged so funds move directly from one bank account to the other.

Step 5: Records Maintenance

After payment is arranged/issued, the records are maintained, probably in a supplier database of sorts. Future shipments will be compared to the database for future negotiations, checking of details, etc. This is also where part of the Spend Analysis (Step One in the Strategic Sourcing) comes from.

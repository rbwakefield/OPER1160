The Role of Inspection

Remember in our previous unit when we mentioned that companies need to consider if the product or process needs to be testable?

To ensure products are produced with little variation, the process and its many facets are inspected to ensure everything is up to standard. This could involve measurement, tasting, smelling, touching or weighing.

Inspection finds only deficiencies or defects. Inspection is expensive and does not add value to the end product but it is a vehicle for improving the system (Lopers et al., 2016, p. 99). The critical questions are 1) where to inspect and 2) when to inspect.

The OM in Action: Maple Leaf Foods Inc. in your text (Lopers et al., 2016, p. 99) is a good but sad reminder of the importance of quality control and inspection, especially in the food industry.

Source Inspection (or source control) is considered the best type of inspection and builds on the concept of employee empowerment. It means each employee self-checks his own work. Inspections can be aided by checklists or “fail-safe” controls called poka-yokes.

Poka-Yokes

These are simple but effective tools or signals to prevent errors. For instance, at the airlines they have you insert your luggage in a frame to ensure it truly is a carry-on bag. Or, when unleaded fuel was introduced they changed the size of the fuel inlet so the nozzle wouldn’t insert. Forgotten to fasten your seatbelt and had the annoying chime go off?

That’s a poka-yoke.

Service industries also require inspections but the questions of where and when to inspect are different for processes—think of a receptionist answering phones, or a surgeon operating on a patient or a waiter serving customers in a restaurant.

Total Quality Management (TQM)

TQM refers to a quality emphasis that encompasses the entire organization right from the first supplier of materials, through all the shipping, processing, packaging, selling, and right to the end consumer. It stresses a commitment by management to have a continuing, company-wide, drive toward excellence in all aspects of products and services that are important to the customer and to improve profitability for the company.

We’re going to look at some of the concepts for an effective TQM program;

Concepts of TQM

Continuous Improvement

The end goal is perfection, which is never achieved but always sought. A circular model as shown here is the PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) Cycle that stresses the ongoing nature of continuous improvement.

Concepts of TQM

Six Sigma

It is a comprehensive system – a strategy, a discipline and a set of tools. It utilizes a five-step process improvement model called DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) and it concentrates on eliminating defects and reducing variability.

Concepts of TQM

Employee Empowerment

TQM emphasizes that the people working on a system or process on a daily basis are the ones that understand it better than anyone else so it means involving employees in every step of a process.

Concepts of TQM

Benchmarking

The idea is to select a demonstrated standard of products, services, costs, or practices that represent the very best performance then use it to compare your own performance. There are two types – competitive (looking at other firms in your own industry) and process benchmarking (non-competitive examination of processes). Your text OM in Action-A Hospital Benchmarks Against the Ferrari Racing Team? on page 91 (Lopers et al., 2016, p. 91) is a great example of a process benchmarking exercise that helped improve the handoff process between departments.

Concepts of TQM

Just-in-Time (JIT)

This philosophy is continuously looking to improve the process to cut costs and shrink lead times so you produce or deliver goods when needed. It avoids using inventory as a cushion to cover up errors or problems in the system.

Concepts of TQM

Knowledge of Tools

To empower employees and implement TQM as a continuing effort, everyone must be trained in the techniques of TQM. Peter Drucker is famous for saying “If you can’t measure it, you can’t improve it.” We’ll examine some of these important measuring tools in the next unit.

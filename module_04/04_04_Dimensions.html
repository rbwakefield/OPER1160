Product and Service Design

Dimensions of new product and service design

When a customer is asked what is important when a company adds a new product or service they will typically name things like:

  • Functionality
  • Aesthetics
  • Ease of use
  • Cost

…and yes, these are important when designing the product/service. What are some of your favourite products and why? Check out some of these product design winners (Fast Company & Inc, 2017).

Companies must also think of the following six things with an internal focus (Lopers et al., 2016, p. 64):

  • Repeatability of the process to produce
  • Testability of the product/service
  • Serviceability of the design
  • Production volumes
  • Product costs
  • Match design with existing capabilities

Let’s look at each of these essential dimensions of product or service design.

Repeatability

A company needs to consistently produce and deliver the same product or service. They want a robust design that will not be affected by minor variations in the process. For instance, the design should be strong enough to be produced by different operators, in different humidity or temperature levels.

Testability

A product or service needs to be tested at various stages rather than just at the completion when, if wrong, would have to be scrapped or completely redone. Testing or quality checking is a non-value added function so you want to minimize the cost of testing and possible waste in case some part of it is bad.

Serviceability

If a product is expected to be serviced or repaired, companies need to consider this in their product design. Lamborghini cars are an excellent example of a product that is not easily serviced. For many models, an oil change means the engine must be dropped out first.

Product volumes

A company has to consider how something is made and how many can be made. If it is so detailed that only a few can be produced at a time, it will limit the sales. Or, if it can’t be inventoried that will also limit the volume of sales.

Product costs

Choices that companies make about the raw materials or features of a service will directly influence the final cost. It is important to not only consider the obvious costs but also hidden ones. Things like packaging needs that might increase the pricing need to be considered.

Match design with existing capabilities

When a company is designing a new product they need to consider its “fit” in the company. Does it build on their core competencies? Is the capital investment in equipment in place? Are the employees trained or do they have expertise in this “new way”?

Consider a company that makes wooden Muskoka chairs. If the marketing department insists they should change to lighter, plastic chairs would that be a good fit? No, the plastic extrusion equipment is far different from the lathes and saws required for wood and the carpenters they currently employ would be hard pressed to have expertise with these new products and processes.

One Elmira, Ontario service company that expanded building on their existing capabilities was Earthscape. They were residential landscape designers but expanded into new product lines (playgrounds) by building on what they knew – wood, stone, and dirt rather than the metal and plastic playgrounds common in many areas.

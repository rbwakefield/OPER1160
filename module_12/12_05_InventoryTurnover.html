Managing Inventory

Inventory Turnover & Weeks Supply

Now that we have examined different reasons for maintaining inventory, we need to look at the main metrics to measure the performance of this expensive investment—inventory turnover and weeks supply.

Inventory Turnover

We examine how many times our inventory investment is used over the period of a year. It will depend on the usage of the inventory and the type of business.

Examples of Annual Inventory Turnover rates

Inventory turns = ( Cost of goods sold Inventory investment ) Inventory turns = left ( {Cost of goods sold} over {Inventory investment} right )
Examples of Annual Inventory Turnover rates
Fresh Produce
104 Xs (twice a week average)
Hardware Store
5 Xs
Car Manufacturer
150 Xs

Weeks of Supply

With this measurement we look at inventory usage by looking at how many weeks of supply we have on hand.

Weeks of supply = Inventory investment / average weekly Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

OR Weeks of supply = 52 weeks / Inventory turns

Note: If not stated, use 52 weeks. Sometimes, due to shutdowns or holidays, weeks may be less.
May also be expressed as Days of supply = 365 days / Inventory turns.

Calculation Excercise

Net revenue
$32.5m
Cost of goods sold
$14.2m
Inventory:
Raw material: $0.74m
WIP inventory $.11m
Finished goods $.84m
Total inventory
$1.69m
Inventory turnover
= COGS / Inventory investment
= 14.2 / 1.69 = 8.4 turns/year
Weeks of supply
= inventory investment / average weekly COGS
= 1.69 / ($14.2 / 52) = 1.69 / .273 = 6.19 weeks of supply
OR
Weeks of supply
= 52 weeks / (turns/year)
= 52 / 8.4 = 6.19 weeks of supply

If you wanted to see Days of Supply = 365 / 8.4 turns = 43.34 days of supply.

Inventory Turns and Weeks Supply are related

It is important to realize that these two calculations are used on an ongoing basis by inventory managers and they are both measuring the rate of usage of the inventory.

52 turns per year… means about 1 week of stock is on hand.

12 turns per year… means there is about 4 weeks of supply on hand.

Importance of carrying the right amount of inventory

It is important to carry the right amount of inventory. Too much, the company will incur additional holding costs such as insurance, rent on too much space, obsolescence, interest on the money borrowed, security, etc.

Too little, they might have to order more often than usual and ordering costs add up.

Plus, the third thing managers are always juggling is Customer Satisfaction. If you don’t have the stock on hand when demanded, customers might be upset or even go elsewhere and the company will lose the sale. The company might also spend extra time and resources to track down the piece somewhere else and expedite it in.

So, having the right amount of inventory at the right time is important!

Note: In a service company you don’t have the option of building inventory, so you need to have excess capacity or personnel available to meet customer requirements. There is a cost to excess capacity or excess inventory that impacts profitability.

Economic Order Quantity – amount to order each time

In a continuous review system, the amount to order each time is calculated in advance and remains the same amount each time. Remember the yellow lines in the Continuous Review System Scenarios diagram?

This Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) is a tradeoff between yearly holding costs and ordering costs. If you hold less, you will order more often. If you order fewer times but larger quantities, then your holding costs will be high. So, the EOQ minimizes the holding costs and ordering costs for an item. We do need to know the following to calculate the EOQ quantity (Q):

  • D – total demand for the year (units NOT sales revenue)
  • S – cost to place a single order
  • H – cost to hold one unit in inventory for a year

Notice you don’t need to worry about the value of the item and the lead time is assumed to be constant. This equation does not take into consideration volume discounts or widely swinging customer demand. It is not perfect but it is a solid indication of the quantity to order each time for cycle stock for independent demand items in a continuous review system.

EOQ = Q x = 2 DS H EOQ = Q^{x} = sqrt{ {2DS} over {H} }

Notice you don’t need to worry about the value of the item and the lead time is assumed to be constant. Notice that in Figure 1 the total cost does not vary that much when in close proximity to the lowest total cost or EOQ. The EOQ is therefore a great place to start when choosing quantities for ordering. There are often many other factors (Ex: carton or skid load quantities) which may result in the actual quantity ordered being close to but not matching exactly the EOQ.

Example: Kevin has an internet company that sells chocolates. He sells 100,000 truffles per year. The annual holding cost per unit is $3 and the cost to place an order is $75. What is the economic order quantity?

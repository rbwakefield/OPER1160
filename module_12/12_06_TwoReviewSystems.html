Managing Inventory

Two Review Systems

Inventory must be monitored or reviewed and depending on the type of inventory and its usage, it might be done by a Periodic review system or a Continuous review system.

Periodic

This is a simple approach for managing independent demand inventory. See Figure 1 that shows a company will check the inventory at set periods of time and restock to a predetermined restocking level (R).

  • (R) is the restocking level
  • (Q) is the quantity needed to bring back to that restocking level.
  • (I) is the amount on hand at the time of review.

In the following example, the stock is checked every 2 weeks (I) and then the needed quantity (Q) is added to bring it up to the restocking level (R).

Click for a larger version

Figure 1. Example of Periodic Review System. Adapted from Lopers et al., 2016, page 189

Q = R - I

Notice how just after week 5, the company runs out of the cycle stock and it is not noted until week 6 when the stock is counted and replenished. In this system, safety stock is included by raising the replenishment (R) level. This system is best suited when constantly monitoring this stock (usually low-value) makes no economic sense. For this, it is okay to keep a large amount of stock and still possibly stock out. Think of a vending machine or racks of chips.

Example: The potato chip sales rep arrives. He checks the dill pickle chips and sees you have room to stock (R) 24 bags and there are 6 bags (I) on the shelf. What is the periodic quantity (Q) needed?

Q = R - I

Q = 24 – 6 Q = 18. Therefore, he puts in 18 bags to bring it up to the replenishment level.

Continuous Review System

A Continuous Review System for independent demand items means the inventory is constantly tracked. When the stock drops to a predetermined reorder point (R), a fixed order quantity (Q) is ordered. The time period varies—it is ordered when the quantity on hand falls to the reorder point. See Figure 2 for the pattern with a constant demand rate (d).

R = ddL + SS

(R = Demand during LT + Safety Stock)

Click for a larger version

Figure 2. Continuous Review System with constant demand. Adapted from Lopers et al., 2016, pg 191

The size of the replenishment order is calculated in advance based on EOQ with factors like predicted demand but also the costs to hold stock and place an order. Shortly we will talk about EOQ (Economic Order Quantity).

The other important factor is the Reorder Point and it is the demand during the leadtime plus safety stock. R = ddL + SS.

Example: Demand (d) averages 100 staplers per week. The supplier leadtime (L) is 2 weeks. The safety stock (SS) is calculated at 20 staplers. Calculate the reorder point.

Q = R - I

R = ddL + SS = (100) (2) + 20 = 220 Therefore, when stock drops to 220 staplers, the company should reorder the predetermined amount (Q).

Your Turn…

A specialty chocolate retailer sells 50 kgs of chocolate almond bark per day. The Swiss supplier has a 10 day lead time. The safety stock is 50 kgs of chocolate. Calculate the reorder point.

Check Your Answer

R = ddL + SS = (50) (10) + 50 = 550 Therefore, when stock drops to 550 kgs, the retailer should reorder.

This specialty chocolate retailers sells 520 kgs of truffles per year. There is a 2 week lead time. The safety stock is 15 kgs of truffles. Calculate the reorder point.

Check Your Answer

R = ddL + SS = (520/52 weeks) (2) + 15 = 20 + 15 = 35 kgs. When it reaches 35 kgs, reorder. Note: Always make sure the demand is in the same time frame as the leadtime. That is why we converted the yearly sales to a weekly sales amount. (520/52).

Warehousing Solutions

In today’s world of having the right parts at the right place at the right time, to minimize inventory levels, and improve customer satisfaction, warehousing is an important, strategic element in supply chain strategy.

This video of an Amazon warehouse equal to 59 football fields with over 1 million different items is a good example you should check out.

Various warehousing solutions allow companies more operational flexibility, shortened lead times and lower inventory carrying costs. (Lopers, Watson, Hill, 2016, p. 155). Let’s look at some different types of warehousing solutions and their benefits. Use the chart below to visualize each of these.

Consolidation warehousing allows a central location to pull together loads of materials from various sources and combining them into larger, therefore more economical shipping loads. Stock may be pulled from the warehouse storage or straight from the incoming small shipments and put into multimodal containers or combined into full truckloads (TL) or consolidated truckloads called less than truckloads (LTL) made up of items from a number of sources. The idea is to improve the weight and space utilization during shipping out.

Cross-Docking warehouses receive the consolidated shipments and break them down into smaller outgoing shipments often to various local points. At no time is the stock put away. If the large, incoming shipment came from a single source it is often called break-bulk warehousing.

Consolidation of loads to move larger quantities on and crossdocking to split up large quantities to combined loads to multiple locations. This chart is available as a PDF .

Hub-and-spoke systems means “hubs” are strategically placed to sort and transfer the materials on its way. Between the hubs, larger transport units can be used (consolidated shipments) and then at the hubs, the cargo can be cross-docked and sent out on the “spokes” to local customers on smaller vehicles. Stock is not warehoused at a hub…it is merely a transfer point. Think of airlines and their main “hub” airports with smaller planes delivering their passengers out on “spokes” to less travelled points.

Hubs for material transfers will be located near large airports, water ports or large highway intersections. Cross-docking and consolidation warehouses will instead be located close to the original shipping or final destination points.

Postponement warehousing

…allows companies to bring stock close to its main operations or customers then repackage, relabel or assemble it into its final format for delivery when it is needed. An example would be practiced by Dell computers. They bring all the computer components to a warehouse then assemble the customized computer after its ordered. It offers them lower finished goods inventory levels and quick delivery to end customers.

Assortment warehouses

…bring a wide variety of stock close to its end destination. It might be the extra safety stock or seldom ordered items but companies need it close by just in case but it avoids taking up expensive selling space. From this closeby location it can be accessed much quicker than ordering from the supplier.

Spot warehouses

…are often trucks or temporary structures erected to carry seasonal or short-term stock where we are anticipating a spike in demand for a short time….like spring-time gardening inventory. It helps companies avoid the capital costs of building permanent warehousing that might go unused during slower times.

Logistics Strategy

With all the moving parts and expenses in the logistics part of a business, it is important for companies to form an overall logistics strategy.

Remember strategy determines “how” a company can meet its mission. Will it compete on differentiation, cost leadership or responsiveness? Each competitive strategy will lead to different logistical strategies and all the pieces need to fit together to support the overall business strategy.

Some questions to consider

Is logistics a core competency for the company?

If it is not the company’s specialty, maybe they should let a 3PL or 4PL company take over that side of the business. Specialists might be able to do it better. For instance, Rome Transportation is one of our Canadian global logistics management corporations.

Is flexibility required?

If you might change suppliers, customers, locations or if money could be used in other ways to respond to market changes and customer demands, maybe it is better to let another company invest in the expensive warehouses, truck systems, and material handling equipment. An example would be Erb Transport in New Hamburg which provides a variety of refrigerated transportation and warehousing solutions.

Does it need to be controlled by you?
Is responsiveness, as in getting it to the end customer on time, one of the competitive advantages? If so, giving over control of the logistics might not be wise.
Do you have a high enough demand to invest in a system yourself?
Is there enough demand for the infrastructure or will it sit idle for part of the year or incur high expenses because loads don’t fill a full truck or container?
Do you need to have dedicated or ad hoc transportation?
For dedicated needs, but not enough to have your own, you might hire a contract carrier (long term agreement) or a 3PL provider. For more sporadic or ad hoc needs, you would call a common carrier that handles shipments when needed.
